I have reviewed somewhere north of two hundred Asia itineraries over the past quarter century. Most of them are garbage. This one is not garbage. This one was built by someone who actually eats.

The routing matters. Hong Kong to Taipei to Singapore is the correct sequence. You descend from Cantonese precision into Taiwanese chaos-warmth, then land in Singapore's manicured intensity for the comedown. Reversing it kills the emotional arc. The builder of this itinerary understands narrative structure, which tells me they understand travel.

Hong Kong: The Foundation

The Rosewood for four nights is the right call. The Manor Suite views over Victoria Harbour at golden hour still make my chest tight after all these years. The one-night pivot to Tai O Heritage Hotel is inspired — that converted 1902 colonial police station only has nine rooms, and in late June you will hear the pink dolphins before dawn if you leave your window open. Book Room 8; it faces the channel.

The street food circuit is nearly flawless. Oi Man Sang in Sham Shui Po is the real deal — and here is something I have never written publicly: the second window station consistently produces better wok hei than the first, because the cross-draft from the alley angles the flame differently against the wok. Stand there. Watch. You will see it.

The snake soup at Ser Wong Fun is a power move. Half my New York friends would refuse. That is precisely why it belongs here.

The HKSAR Eve fireworks over the harbour on June 30 are a genuine once-a-year spectacle. Watching from the Rosewood rooftop with a Negroni while a million people pack the waterfront below — that is the kind of moment that makes a New York penthouse feel provincial.

Taipei: The Heart

This is where the itinerary becomes genuinely special. Fu Hang Soy Milk at 5:30 AM is non-negotiable, and the fact that it is listed tells me the builder has actually stood in that fluorescent-lit stairwell clutching a number ticket while half-asleep. Get the dan bing with the shao bing you tiao. Do not let anyone talk you into the "lighter" options.

The Mandarin Oriental for three nights followed by Villa 32 in Beitou is the best hotel pairing in this entire itinerary. Villa 32 has only five rooms and sits above private hot spring pools fed by volcanic sulfur springs. After five days of night market humidity, you sink into 42-degree water surrounded by camphor trees and your entire nervous system recalibrates. Late June means occasional afternoon downpours — but at Villa 32, rain on the forest canopy while you are submerged in an outdoor spring is not a flaw. It is the entire point.

EMBERS is the correct fine dining pick. Chef Wes Kuo's indigenous tasting menu — using maqaw pepper, millet, and wild boar — is unlike anything in Manhattan, and that is what this trip should be about.

Singapore: The Finale

Raffles for two nights is ceremonial and correct. The pivot to Mandai Rainforest Treehouse is bold and I love it — waking up in a canopy structure near the Night Safari, hearing hornbills at sunrise, is about as far from a NYC studio apartment as human engineering allows.

Midnight durian in Geylang is the kind of entry that separates a good itinerary from a great one. Go to the stalls, ask for Mao Shan Wang grade A, eat it on the plastic stool on the sidewalk at 1 AM. If you are not slightly uncomfortable, you are not doing it right.

Soup Tulang at 2 AM — sucking spiced marrow from mutton bones through a straw while the city sleeps — this is the moment your New York friends will not believe when you describe it. This is why you came.

Three Substitutions That Make This Legendary

  • Hong Kong: Replace the Peak Tram with the Old Peak Road hike up. Earn your view. Dodge the 40-minute July queue that smells like sunscreen and impatience.
  • Taipei: Replace KTV night with Mountain and Sea House, a private-kitchen supper club in Da'an serving twelve-course Hakka banquets to eight guests. The single hardest reservation in Taipei. Worth every ounce of effort.
  • Singapore: Replace Haw Par Villa with a Katong Peranakan laksa crawl through the shophouses, ending at 328 Katong Laksa. That neighborhood tells you more about Singapore's soul than any theme park ever could.

Verdict

This is comfortably in the top five percent of Asia itineraries I have reviewed. It balances luxury with grit, spectacle with intimacy, and famous names with genuine discoveries. The late June timing gives you HKSAR fireworks, Taipei mango season at its peak, and Singapore before the August haze from Sumatra. The hotel pairings — particularly the Rosewood-to-Tai O and Mandarin Oriental-to-Villa 32 pivots — show a sophistication that most luxury travelers never reach.

Go. Eat everything. Sweat through your shirt in Sham Shui Po. Fall asleep in a volcanic hot spring. Suck marrow through a straw at 2 AM. And when you land back at JFK and someone asks how the trip was, just smile. They would not understand anyway.

— Anthony Chen writes the Asia Circuit column and has not had a bad meal on purpose since 1998.
